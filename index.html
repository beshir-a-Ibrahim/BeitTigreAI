üìö Overview

The Tigre language, spoken primarily in Eritrea and eastern Sudan, is widely recognized by linguists as the closest living relative of Classical Geez‚Äîa historically significant Semitic language that, while no longer spoken, remains central to religious and scholarly traditions. Unlike Geez, Tigre is a vibrant, actively spoken language with a growing digital footprint. It is increasingly represented across modern media platforms, including Wikipedia, Mozilla Common Voice, Tatoeba, newspapers, literature, and broadcast media such as radio and television.

Despite this vitality, Tigre has remained significantly underrepresented in the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP). This work aims to bridge that gap by introducing a comprehensive set of open resources and tools to support NLP research and development for the language. Moreover, due to the scarcity of accessible learning materials for Classical Geez, Tigre serves as a crucial living linguistic bridge. Advances in Tigre NLP can therefore yield valuable insights into Geez, offering pathways toward its computational understanding and potential revitalization.

We gratefully acknowledge the Tigre-speaking community for their enduring commitment to preserving and promoting their linguistic heritage. Their contributions‚Äîespecially through voice recordings, text corpora, and translation efforts‚Äîhave been instrumental in the data collection that underpins this work. Leveraging these community-driven resources, we have developed a suite of NLP models for Tigre, including:

A Large Language Model (LLM) (Llama 3.2-based, in progress)

Machine Translation systems

Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR)

Text-to-Speech (TTS) synthesis

fastText vector representations for both Tigre and Geez

These efforts reflect broader recognition of the language‚Äôs importance: Tigre and Geez are now included in the Meta/UNESCO Language Technology Partner Program, and Tigre is currently in the process of being integrated into Google Translate‚Äîmarking significant milestones in the digital inclusion of both languages.
